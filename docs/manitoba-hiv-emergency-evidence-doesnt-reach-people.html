Manitoba’s HIV Emergency: When Evidence Doesn’t Reach People | Ayel L. R. Batac

A reflection on Manitoba’s HIV public health emergency, access to prevention and treatment, harm reduction, and why evidence only matters when it reaches people in real life.

Manitoba’s HIV Emergency: When Evidence Doesn’t Reach People

May 9, 2026

TL;DR
  • Manitoba has declared a public health emergency in response to rising HIV cases.
  • HIV is preventable and treatable. People living with HIV who are on effective treatment can live long, healthy lives and cannot sexually transmit HIV when they are virally suppressed.
  • Manitoba already has important HIV prevention and care tools in place.
  • The challenge is whether people can reach those tools early enough and in ways that keep them connected to care.
  • This emergency is a systems warning: prevention and treatment only work when care is reachable, trusted, and possible to return to.

Manitoba’s HIV emergency is hard to sit with because HIV is not a mystery anymore.

We know how to prevent HIV. We know how to treat HIV. And still, cases are rising.

On May 7, 2026, Manitoba declared a public health emergency in response to rising HIV cases. New diagnoses increased from 90 in 2019 to 328 in 2025. In 2024, Manitoba’s HIV diagnosis rate was more than three-and-a-half times the national rate. The burden isn’t evenly distributed across the province: diagnosis rates are highest in Prairie Mountain Health and Northern Health, while Winnipeg has the largest number of diagnoses.

I’m from Winnipeg, so this feels close to home. And because I first lived in the Philippines before moving to Canada, I’m careful about the word “access”.

Living across two health system contexts taught me that a service can exist on paper and still be difficult to reach in real life. Access is not simply the presence of a program. It’s whether the path to that program is realistic for the person who needs it.

In the Philippines, I saw how a clinic visit could become difficult once cost, transportation, and uncertainty about the health system entered the picture. In Canada, the barriers may look different, but the core question is familiar: can people actually reach the care that public health tells them is available?

That’s why I don’t read Manitoba’s HIV emergency as a failure of HIV science. The science is strong. The harder question is whether people can reach the tools we already have early enough and in ways that make it possible to stay connected to care.

HIV has changed, but stigma still gets in the way

For many people, HIV is still surrounded by fear, shame, and outdated assumptions. But HIV prevention and treatment have changed dramatically.

A person living with HIV who takes treatment and maintains viral suppression cannot sexually transmit HIV. The Public Health Agency of Canada describes viral suppression as a viral load of less than 200 copies of virus per millilitre of blood, usually measured regularly as part of clinical care.

Stigma is not just a social issue. It follows people into the very moments when they are deciding whether care feels safe enough to seek. If public communication around an HIV emergency increases fear without increasing understanding, it can make the response harder.

The message needs to be clear: HIV is preventable and treatable, and people living with HIV deserve care without shame.

A program existing is not enough

Manitoba is not starting from nothing.

The province has already invested in HIV prevention and care. Through the Manitoba Enhanced Pharmacare Program, key HIV prevention and treatment medications are available at no cost. Manitoba has also supported care models that work closer to the communities most affected, including the Program to Access Treatment for HIV and Support (PATHS).

That work matters. People across the province have been working on HIV prevention and care for years, often in difficult conditions and with communities that have already experienced too much stigma. The emergency declaration should build on that work, not erase it. It should help Manitoba move faster and be more honest about where people are still being missed.

Pre-exposure prophylaxis (PrEP) is medication that HIV-negative people can take before exposure to prevent HIV. Post-exposure prophylaxis (PEP) can prevent HIV after a possible exposure, but only if it’s started quickly, within 72 hours. Treatment is also prevention. When people living with HIV are diagnosed early and supported to stay on medication, they can live long and healthy lives. When they are virally suppressed, they also cannot sexually transmit HIV.

These tools only work when people can actually use them. Even strong prevention and treatment options lose impact when people encounter them too late, never hear about them, or cannot stay connected to care.

The issue is not that we lack tools. It is that too many people still meet the system too late, or not at all. It is not enough to know that something works. We have to ask whether it works for the people who need it most, in the places where they actually live and seek care.

Why harm reduction belongs in the HIV response

Public health messaging often tells people how to protect themselves from HIV. Those messages matter, but they become incomplete when they are separated from people’s real lives.

Manitoba’s HIV context makes this especially clear. The province has identified both sexual and injection-related transmission as important parts of the current picture. It has also recognized that HIV risk is shaped by the conditions around people’s lives, especially when instability, stigma, substance use, and mistrust of healthcare overlap.

That means harm reduction is not separate from HIV prevention. It is HIV prevention.

In plain language, harm reduction means meeting people where they are. It makes care safer to approach, reduces the risk of infection, and creates a path into testing and treatment without requiring people to be perfect before they are helped.

A person may understand that testing is important and still avoid care because they have been judged before. Someone may want PrEP but have no regular provider to ask. Someone may start HIV treatment and still struggle to stay connected because life is unstable or because healthcare has not always felt safe.

When this happens again and again, it is not just about individual choices. It tells us something about the system people are trying to navigate.

When evidence does not reach people

This access problem is not unique to Manitoba. Around the world, HIV cases can rise even when effective prevention and treatment tools already exist.

The problem is not always that we do not know what to do. Often, the problem is that care does not reach people in time, or in a way they can trust.

In the Philippines, where I grew up for part of my life, UNAIDS has described the country as experiencing the fastest-growing HIV epidemic in the Asia-Pacific region. Estimated new HIV infections increased by 543% from 2010 to 2023, and 33% of new infections occurred among youth aged 15 to 24.

Fiji offers another example of the same implementation challenge. The Government of Fiji declared an HIV outbreak in January 2025 after a sharp increase in newly diagnosed cases. UNAIDS emphasized that the response needed to be stigma-free, community-led, and grounded in both prevention and harm reduction..

I’m not saying Manitoba is the same as the Philippines or Fiji. The contexts are very different. But the lesson is familiar: evidence alone is not enough if systems do not help people use it.

After the declaration

A public health emergency declaration matters. It can focus attention and make it possible to move faster. But the real test begins after the announcement.

For Manitoba, the next step is not simply to tell people that the tools exist. The next step is to make care easier to use when people need it.

HIV testing should feel routine rather than shameful. PrEP conversations should happen before someone has to know the right words to ask. PEP needs to be reachable quickly, including after regular clinic hours. HIV treatment programs need to be flexible enough to keep people connected when daily life is unstable.

Harm reduction, Indigenous-led services, and community-led care should not be treated as add-ons. They are part of the infrastructure of an effective HIV response.

Healthcare providers can also reframe what a missed appointment means. Sometimes it’s not “non-adherence”. Sometimes it’s a sign that the model of care is not flexible enough for the person’s life.

For the broader public, the task is different: replace fear with facts. HIV is not spread through casual contact. People living with HIV are not a threat to their communities.

The bottom line

Manitoba’s HIV emergency should not be read as a failure of individual morality or a failure of biomedical knowledge. It is better understood as a systems warning.

The tools are already here. The challenge is making sure they reach people before the moment to intervene is lost.

That is what access means. It’s not just having a program listed on a website. It’s whether care feels possible to seek and safe enough to return to when life gets complicated.

Access is not a soft issue or a secondary concern. In Manitoba’s HIV emergency, it is what will determine whether prevention and treatment work in real life.

-Ayel
