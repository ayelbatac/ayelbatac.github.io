Syphilis Self-Testing: Access Is the Intervention | Ayel L. R. Batac

A reflection on syphilis self-testing, diagnostic systems, and why access to testing remains a public health challenge even in well-resourced settings.

Syphilis Self-Testing: Access Is the Intervention

January 2, 2026

TL;DR
  • Syphilis is curable, yet cases continue to rise.
  • The problem is not the lack of treatment. It’s whether people can get tested early enough.
  • Centralized diagnostic systems work well for many people, but they can still miss those who face the greatest barriers to care.
  • Decentralized approaches, including syphilis self-testing, have helped expand access in many settings.
  • Self-testing does not replace healthcare. It works best when people know what to do next and can actually get care afterward.

Syphilis is curable. That is exactly why rising cases should unsettle us.

When a treatable infection continues to spread, we have to look beyond the medicine itself. The harder question is whether testing systems are actually reaching the people who need them.

I think about this often because I have seen how differently testing and care work in the Philippines and Canada. Living first in the Philippines and later in Canada taught me that a service can exist on paper and still be difficult to reach in real life. I noticed that long before I knew how to describe it as a public health problem.

In the Philippines, I saw how much care could depend on what was nearby and what people could afford. In Canada and the United States, STI testing is usually routed through more formal clinic and laboratory systems. Those systems can be strong, but they also shape whether testing happens early enough and whether people can return for care.

That perspective shaped a recent invited commentary I co-authored in the Journal of Clinical Microbiology with Drs. Michael Marks, Joseph Tucker, and Rosanna Peeling, whose work has influenced how many of us think about diagnostics and self-testing.

In the commentary, we focused on a simple but difficult question:

Who is being missed by the way we currently test for syphilis?

When testing depends on the clinic

In Canada, STI testing usually means entering the formal healthcare system. A person gets tested through a clinic or hospital, the sample goes to a laboratory, and treatment depends on the result getting back to the right place at the right time.

This model has real strengths. It gives clinicians reliable results and creates a clear pathway for follow-up care. For many people, it works well.

But it also assumes that people can get to care and feel safe using it. That is not always true.

For someone who lives far from services, expects judgment, or has had bad experiences with healthcare, clinic-based testing may be technically available but practically out of reach. A system can be strong on paper and still miss the people who need it most.

That is why rising syphilis rates are not only about treatment. They are also about how testing is organized.

What decentralized testing can teach us

In many parts of Asia and Africa, decentralized testing did not emerge simply because it was convenient. It emerged because the usual clinic-and-lab model was not enough.

Rapid tests can be used outside large laboratories and give results quickly. That matters when travel is difficult, when people may not return for follow-up, or when stigma makes clinic visits feel unsafe.

There is an important trade-off. Rapid syphilis tests are useful for screening, but reactive results still need confirmatory testing. That does not mean the standard is lower. It means the testing strategy is being shaped around the reality of who can be reached.

I see this clearly through my work in China.

In one randomized trial among men who have sex with men, distributing dual HIV and syphilis self-testing kits through social networks increased testing compared with site-based testing. People with reactive results still accessed confirmatory testing, and the study did not find evidence of social harm.

Evidence from Zimbabwe points in a similar direction. In a study comparing facility-based syphilis testing with community-based self-testing among gay, bisexual, and transgender men, self-testing increased uptake and lowered the cost per test for users.

These studies do not mean self-testing solves everything. They show something more practical: testing reaches more people when it is easier to use.

For me, that is the central lesson. The design of a testing system affects who gets diagnosed and who gets care.

Where syphilis self-testing fits

Syphilis self-testing allows a person to test outside a clinic, usually with a finger-prick blood sample, and receive a result within minutes.

In 2024, the first at-home, over-the-counter syphilis self-test was authorized in the United States. That matters because high-income countries have long relied on centralized laboratory testing for STI diagnosis.

But a self-test is not the same as a diagnosis. Current syphilis self-tests detect treponemal antibodies, which can remain positive even after a past infection has been treated. A reactive result still needs confirmatory laboratory testing and clinical follow-up.

That point is important. Self-testing should not leave people on their own.

Used well, it can be a first step into care for people who might not walk into a clinic. Used poorly, it can create confusion, anxiety, or a result with nowhere to go.

Experience with HIV self-testing offers a useful lesson. The test itself is only one part of the intervention. What matters is whether people know what to do next and can actually get care afterward.

Syphilis self-testing has to follow the same principle.

Designing testing around real life

Self-testing can still leave people behind if we are not careful.

If tests are only sold online or in pharmacies, they may never reach the people who face the biggest barriers to clinic-based testing. If a person gets a reactive result but has no affordable way to confirm it or receive treatment, the system has still failed them.

The answer is not to replace laboratories. It is to make testing easier to reach while keeping strong pathways into care.

That means thinking beyond the test kit: how people will get it, what happens after a reactive result, and whether confirmatory testing feels reachable.

Those questions matter because syphilis is not difficult to treat. The harder problem is often getting people tested and connected to care early enough.

The bigger question

Health systems often work best for people who can move through them easily. But many people do not experience care that way.

People do not avoid STI testing for one reason. Sometimes it is shame. Sometimes it is work, distance, or not having a regular provider. Sometimes it is because healthcare has not felt safe before.

A diagnostic system that only works for people who can comfortably enter the clinic will always miss someone.

That is why syphilis self-testing is worth taking seriously. Not because it is perfect, and not because it can replace clinical care. It matters because it asks a different question: what would testing look like if we designed it around the people least likely to be reached by the current system?

The bottom line

Syphilis self-testing will not solve rising syphilis rates on its own.

But it can help reach people who are not being reached by clinic-based testing alone. It can make testing feel more private and less dependent on whether someone is willing or able to enter the healthcare system first.

The promise is not the test by itself. The promise is what becomes possible when testing is easier to reach and care is ready to respond.

-Ayel

Commentary Authors

This commentary was written by a team of researchers whose work spans diagnostics, infectious diseases, and global health, with a shared focus on access, equity, and real-world impact.

Ayel L. R. Batac is a public health researcher at the London School of Hygiene and Tropical Medicine, and a researcher with the University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill and SESH Global, the China hub of the Social Innovation in Health Initiative (TDR, the Special Programme for Research and Training in Tropical Diseases). His research focuses on the social epidemiology of sexually transmitted infections, with an emphasis on inequities in access to testing and prevention among marginalized populations.

Dr. Michael Marks is a Professor of Medicine at the London School of Hygiene and Tropical Medicine and an infectious diseases consultant at the Hospital for Tropical Diseases, University College London Hospitals NHS Foundation Trust. His research focuses on the epidemiology, diagnosis, and control of infectious diseases, including syphilis and other neglected tropical diseases, with particular emphasis on community-based treatment strategies and integrated approaches to disease control.

Dr. Joseph Tucker is a Professor of Global Health at the London School of Hygiene and Tropical Medicine and a Professor of Medicine at the University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill. His work centers on the prevention and control of sexually transmitted infections, including syphilis, and on the use of crowdsourcing, social networks, and community-driven approaches to expand access to testing and care, particularly in global and low-resource settings.

Dr. Rosanna Peeling is an Emeritus Professor of Diagnostics Research at the London School of Hygiene and Tropical Medicine and a global leader in diagnostic innovation. Trained as a medical microbiologist, her work has been foundational in shaping how diagnostics for HIV, syphilis, and other infectious diseases are developed, evaluated, and implemented across diverse global contexts.
