Guidelines Are Global. Access Is Not: Structural Gaps in Anaphylaxis Treatment | Ayel L. R. Batac

A reflection on global variation in anaphylaxis treatment, epinephrine access, and how structural inequities shape emergency care despite international guideline consensus.

Guidelines Are Global. Access Is Not: Structural Gaps in Anaphylaxis Treatment

March 10, 2026

TL;DR
  • Anaphylaxis is life-threatening and requires immediate treatment with epinephrine.
  • International guidelines are clear, but access to recommended treatment varies widely across health systems.
  • New options like intranasal epinephrine expand what is possible.
  • Yet possibility without access is not protection.

Epinephrine, also known as adrenaline, is the first-line treatment for anaphylaxis. That part is clear.

The harder question is whether people can actually get it when they need it.

That question has stayed with me because I have seen how differently medications are accessed in the Philippines and Canada. In the Philippines, I was used to seeing nebulizers in homes and clinics. A nebulizer machine could be bought once, and salbutamol solution was familiar and relatively inexpensive. For many families, that made sense.

Metered-dose inhalers, or “puffers” felt different. They were portable and commonly used in places like Canada, but they also required refills and could feel more expensive over time. In Canada, I saw the opposite pattern: inhalers were routine, while nebulizers were usually reserved for more specific clinical situations.

Neither approach is random. People adapt to the system they are given.

That experience changed how I think about medications. A treatment can be clinically standard and still feel out of reach. What families keep at home is shaped long before an emergency happens.

That is the lens I bring to anaphylaxis.

Anaphylaxis is one of the most time-sensitive emergencies in medicine. Across major allergy guidelines, the recommendation is consistent: epinephrine should be given promptly by intramuscular injection.

But a clear recommendation does not mean practice looks the same everywhere.

That perspective shaped an editorial I wrote on the introduction of epinephrine auto-injectors in mainland China. It also continues to inform my collaborative work with physician and allied health partners across Canada and Australia.

The question I keep coming back to is simple:

If epinephrine is the first-line treatment for anaphylaxis, why does treatment still look so different in practice?

That question led us to examine how anaphylaxis is managed across health systems and to develop a scoping review protocol to guide that work.

What medication access looks like in real life

Guidelines often make treatment sound straightforward. Real emergencies are shaped by what the system has made reachable before the reaction begins.

In many parts of Asia, including the Philippines, epinephrine auto-injectors are not routinely stocked in retail pharmacies. Families may instead rely on ampoules, which require someone to prepare and give the medication correctly under pressure. In other places, the product may exist but still be hard to obtain because of price, supply, or regulation.

This changes what preparedness means. It’s one thing to know that epinephrine is first-line treatment. It’s another thing to have a device nearby and feel prepared to use it.

I explored this issue in Clinical & Experimental Allergy through the introduction of epinephrine auto-injectors in mainland China. In 2023, they were approved for clinical use in the Guangdong-Hong Kong-Macao Greater Bay Area, but broader availability across mainland China remains limited.

An allergic reaction does not change based on geography, but access to first-line treatment often does.

Why practice does not always follow guidelines

It can be tempting to describe differences in anaphylaxis treatment as “non-adherence” to guidelines. Sometimes that may be true. But often, the situation is more complicated.

By the time an emergency happens, the system has already narrowed the options. A clinician can only reach for what has been approved, supplied, and taught. A family can only use what they were able to obtain before the emergency began.

In some settings, evidence suggests that epinephrine is not always given first. Antihistamines or corticosteroids may be used before epinephrine, treatment may be delayed, or a different route may be used. These differences matter because anaphylaxis moves quickly, and delayed treatment can change the course of an emergency.

The allergic reaction begins in the body, but what happens next depends on what the health system has made possible.

That is why new treatment options matter.

A changing landscape: intranasal epinephrine

The treatment landscape for anaphylaxis is beginning to change.

neffy®, a needle-free nasal epinephrine spray, has been approved in the United States and Canada for emergency treatment of severe allergic reactions, including anaphylaxis. For some people, a nasal spray may feel less intimidating than an injection. That matters in an emergency, where hesitation can cost time.

Emerging evidence suggests that intranasal epinephrine can produce epinephrine levels in the body that are comparable to intramuscular delivery in appropriate contexts. Some recent guidance has also begun to discuss intranasal epinephrine as an alternative option in selected clinical situations.

This is real progress. But approval is only the first step.

A new product does not automatically make care more equal. After approval, the practical question is whether the product reaches the people for whom an injection is a barrier. If it remains expensive or concentrated in well-resourced markets, it could widen the very gaps it has the potential to narrow.

The question is not only whether innovation expands what is possible. It is who gets to benefit from it.

Why this work matters

Much of the anaphylaxis literature focuses on how often anaphylaxis happens and what causes it. Those questions matter.

I am also interested in what happens after a guideline is written. Does epinephrine actually become the first treatment people receive, or does practice change depending on where the emergency happens?

These are not abstract questions. They are the difference between a treatment that exists in a guideline and a treatment that is actually reachable during an emergency.

Mapping global variation is not about ranking countries. It’s about understanding why treatment becomes possible in one setting and difficult in another.

When a life-saving treatment exists but people cannot reliably reach it, the problem is bigger than clinical variation. It means the system is deciding, quietly and unevenly, who has a better chance in an emergency.

The bigger question

Emergency care is often assumed to be standardized, especially when the guideline is clear. But emergencies do not happen outside inequality.

Long before an allergic reaction occurs, the system has already shaped the response. It has already shaped what is within reach.

Guidelines matter. But they do not treat anyone unless people can act on them.

No child’s survival should depend on where they were born.

The bottom line

Anaphylaxis is life-threatening. We know what first-line treatment should be. New options, including intranasal epinephrine, are expanding what may be possible.

But consensus alone is not enough.

Until first-line treatment is reachable when and where people need it, guidelines alone will not save lives.

-Ayel
